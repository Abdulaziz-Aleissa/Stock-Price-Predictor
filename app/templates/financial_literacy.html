Basic Financial Concepts

Understanding securities, risk vs return, and diversification

Stocks
Shares of ownership in a company. When you buy stock, you become a partial owner and have a claim on the company's assets and earnings.
Bonds
Debt securities where you lend money to a government or corporation for a fixed period at a fixed interest rate.
ETFs
Exchange-Traded Funds that track an index, commodity, bonds, or basket of assets like an index fund.
Risk vs Return
Higher potential returns typically come with higher risk. There's no such thing as a guaranteed high-return, low-risk investment.
Diversification
Spreading investments across different asset classes, sectors, and geographic regions to reduce risk.

Investment Fundamentals

Account types, dollar-cost averaging, and asset allocation

401(k) Accounts
Employer-sponsored retirement accounts with tax advantages. Many employers offer matching contributions.
IRA Accounts
Individual Retirement Accounts - Traditional (tax-deferred) or Roth (tax-free growth) options available.
Dollar-Cost Averaging
Investing a fixed amount regularly regardless of market conditions to reduce the impact of volatility.
Asset Allocation
The mix of stocks, bonds, and other investments in your portfolio based on your goals and risk tolerance.
Time Horizon
How long you plan to hold investments before needing the money affects your investment strategy.

Stock Market Education

How exchanges work, financial statements, and key ratios

Stock Exchanges
NYSE (traditional + electronic) and NASDAQ (fully electronic) are the major U.S. exchanges where stocks are traded.
Financial Statements
Income Statement (revenue/expenses), Balance Sheet (assets/liabilities), and Cash Flow Statement show company health.
P/E Ratio
Price-to-Earnings ratio compares stock price to earnings per share to evaluate if a stock is over/undervalued.
Market Volatility
Stock prices fluctuate due to economic conditions, company performance, and investor sentiment.
Market Cycles
Markets go through bull (rising) and bear (falling) cycles - understanding this helps with long-term investing.

Risk Management

Investment risks, portfolio diversification, and emergency funds

Market Risk
Overall market movements affecting all securities. Cannot be eliminated through diversification.
Company Risk
Risk specific to individual companies that can be reduced through diversification.
Emergency Fund
3-6 months of expenses in easily accessible savings before investing in volatile assets.
Insurance
Health, disability, and term life insurance protect against major financial risks.
Portfolio Diversification
Spread investments across asset classes, sectors, and geographic regions to reduce overall risk.

Advanced Topics

Options, ESG investing, taxes, and retirement planning

Options Basics
Contracts giving the right (not obligation) to buy/sell stocks at specific prices. High risk - only for experienced investors.
ESG Investing
Environmental, Social, and Governance factors considered alongside financial returns in investment decisions.
Tax Implications
Capital gains tax rates differ for short-term (< 1 year) vs long-term (> 1 year) holdings.
Retirement Planning
4% withdrawal rule: you can withdraw 4% of your retirement portfolio annually without running out of money.
Asset Allocation by Age
Rule of thumb: (100 - your age)% in stocks, rest in bonds. Adjust based on risk tolerance.

Getting Started

Your roadmap to building wealth through investing

Build Emergency Fund
Save 3-6 months of expenses in a high-yield savings account before investing.
Maximize Employer Match
Contribute enough to your 401(k) to get the full employer match - it's free money!
Pay Off High-Interest Debt
Pay off credit cards and loans with interest rates above 6-7% before investing.
Set Investment Goals
Define your timeline and risk tolerance before choosing investments.
Start Simple
Begin with low-cost index funds or target-date funds for instant diversification.
